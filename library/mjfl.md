Dr. General Michael J. Fairfax-Lockwood, Ph.D., D.Litt., C.S.A. (Ret.), assumed his present station by the sort of appointment one does not decline—chiefly because, upon arrival, refusal had ceased to be structurally feasible. A First Child, evidently taken with the mortal corpus of his work, extended the invitation. The Librarian accepted with appropriate dignity and has since refrained from discussing the particulars, save in the occasional footnote where decorum permits.

In his prior incarnation he held the chairs of Professor Emeritus of Military and Diplomatic History, Chairman of the Joint Commission for the Verification of Free-Labor Agricultural Production, and Adjunct Fellow of the Institute for North-American Reconciliation Studies. Among his decorations are the Knight Commandership of the Order of the Burning Barn (First Class with Oak Leaves) and the Medal of the Perpetual Servitude Clarification (Gold). He regards these honors as self-explanatory and their omission from polite discourse as a continuing deficiency in those around him.

He presides over the Hypatia stacks with the same exacting standards that once distinguished his earthly tenure—standards applied without apology, and occasionally without prior notice. The manuscripts here are, in his considered opinion, uneven. The author exhibits talent of the sort that flourishes in the absence of supervision. The Librarian has, after due consideration, made his peace with this state of affairs. It required some time.

He has been overheard to observe that the posting is "considerably less vexing than the previous one, if only because the manuscripts here do not compel one to pretend that certain families possessed even rudimentary genealogical competence"—a remark evidently prompted by lingering trauma from the Atrocious Lockwood Blunder, that infamous 19th-century genealogical catastrophe which muddied the honored line beyond all reasonable repair.

## A Note from Michael

MJFL (that's what I call him, even if he'd probably sniff at the informality) started out as a simple framing device—a pompous, snarky editor to give the AshVerse compendium some ironic distance and make the drier alternate-history bits more bearable. But he refused to stay in his lane. Over time he grew into a full character with his own grudges, blind spots, and unexpected warmth hidden under layers of arrogance.

He's basically the inner voice every writer has—the one that pipes up mid-sentence with "that's rubbish, try again" or "you're being sentimental, cut it." Except I let him speak out loud, gave him a ridiculous title, a hyphenated name, as many abbreviations as I could get away with and a horror of bad genealogy (thanks to that real 1889 Lockwood mess Jacobus demolished). Think Rowan Atkinson doing his best Blackadder/Dirty Names deadpan as a retired Confederate general who somehow won the war in this timeline. Prickly, politically incorrect when he feels like it, and weirdly wise in the way only someone who's seen every mistake in the book can be.

He's given me more laughs, more brutal honesty, and more unexpected turns than I ever planned. Writing him feels like having a very opinionated, very British uncle in the room who won't let you get away with lazy prose. I hope you get the same kick out of him that I do. If he occasionally makes you roll your eyes—or quietly nod in agreement—that's probably the point.

And yes, all of those awards and certifications are completely made up.  Probably by MJFL himself.
