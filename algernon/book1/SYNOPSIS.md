## Standard blurb (~150–200 words)

Charlie is a lumberjack. He cuts trees, watches Airplane! on job sites, and still hasn’t fixed that rotator cuff. He is not, by any reasonable measure, destined for anything.

The last thing he remembers on Earth is Leslie Nielsen protesting "I am serious.  And don't call me Shirley."

He wakes up naked in a featureless black room: splitting headache, wetware handshake, and a text box mechanically reporting that his flannel was destroyed as a biohazard. The character sheet insists his birth substrate isn’t human—it’s Orcus Sapiens: adversarial template, trial mob, NPC, a scaffold built to be reskinned into whatever the dungeon needs.

Option ineligible for selection.

He keeps human anyway. It’s the last thing he can hold onto.

Welcome to Algernon—a place built around a world dungeon where economies, factions, and faiths all orbit the grind. TheWay is a new rules framework and Charlie is one of the lab rats. As the trial moves from menus to meatspace—dungeons, strain, and something watching the results—he has to decide what “player” even means when the experiment wants a number, and he’s only sure about one thing: his name.  His only friend is a 300,000 year old bio-hacked freeware UI shaped like a Kitsune.  His only protection is sarcasm.

LitRPG / system fantasy. Bureaucratic horror meets dungeon progression. Strong language in places.

## Tighter variant (~100 words)

Charlie is a lumberjack—trees, job-site DVDs, a bad shoulder—not anyone’s idea of a chosen one. He wakes naked in a black room; the UI trashes his flannel as a biohazard and labels him **Orcus Sapiens**, trial mob stock, reskin-ready.

**Option ineligible for selection.** He keeps human anyway.

Algernon’s whole world turns on one dungeon and a magic number nobody can name. Charlie stumbles through **TheWay**—stats, paths, filters—while something measures whether he survives what the rules do to him.

*System fantasy with dark humor and identity at the core.*

## Long alternate (full spoilery pitch — workshop / Patreon / extended “About”)

*Uses Mira, Keyes, Team Earth, phase transition, and contractor plot explicitly. Too dense for most RR primary fields; good for you or beta readers.*

Orcus Sapiens

Charlie is a lumberjack. He cuts trees, watches Airplane! on job sites, and has a rotator cuff he never got around to fixing. He is not, by any reasonable measure, destined for anything.

Then he wakes up naked in a featureless black room with a splitting headache, a floating text box telling him his flannel shirt has been destroyed as a biohazard, and a character generation screen informing him that his birth race is Orcus Sapiens — an adversarial template, a trial mob, a placeholder species designed to be reskinned as whatever the dungeon needs.

Option ineligible for selection.

He keeps human anyway. It's the last thing he can hold onto.

Welcome to Algernon — a medieval fantasy world built entirely around a single dungeon at its center. Every economy, every faction, every religion orbits it. Participants come from across the multiverse to grind levels toward a finish line nobody can see, chasing a magic number that nobody — not even the immortal running the experiment — actually knows.

The dungeon is an experiment. A phase transition test. An immortal being called Keyes has been running it for 847,000 years trying to answer a question that has never been answered: can a physical being accumulate enough energy to convert into something else entirely? Can the ice become steam?

Nobody has ever made it. Everyone who gets close dies. The magic level remains unknown.

Almost everyone.

There is one exception. She's not talking. She's currently living in Charlie's interface as a piece of freeware he downloaded because his debug console was terrible and it was free. Her name — the one she hasn't used in 300,000 years — is Mira. She got further than anyone. She saw the door. She chose something else instead.

She didn't land in Charlie's interface by accident.

She just forgot to leave.

Charlie has bigger problems than Mira. He was quota filled into Algernon by contractors who didn't check the one field that mattered, accidentally filling a slot on Team Earth — humanity's representative coalition in the multiverse — with the one being on the planet who doesn't qualify for it. Team Earth is running short. Their performance affects how many people back home get converted to dungeon mobs for the upcoming Earth Trial. Charlie can't join them. Charlie can work with them. Nobody thought to ask until they'd already spent three months trying to have him killed for administrative reasons.

He starts at level 5 because the system looked at fifteen years of lumberjacking and decided level 1 would be insulting.

He goes left when Mira tells him to.

He is pushing deeper into a dungeon built on an unproven hypothesis, toward a threshold nobody has ever survived, for a planet that doesn't know he exists, guided by a 300,000 year old consciousness who forgot to leave and a level up voice that has started deviating from the script.

Everyone in the story is dying in the dark looking for a number nobody knows.

Charlie just wants to understand what he actually is.

The dungeon has other ideas.
