"Elgrin? Ellgrinn? La-gor-in?" He tried to sound the name out, "Al-ger-non? Algernon?"

The last version seemed to click, as if it felt right, though Charlie couldn't figure out why.

A new line caught his eye as the scrolling text paused for just a moment.

There followed a detailed list of what had been removed, his car keys, wallet, basically anything that wasn't part of his physical body had been "REMOVED/DESTROYED".

He looked down, the floating text remained in his field of view. Luckily, Charlie had never had complaints, but it was still a bit awkward.

Charlie was naked and a little surprised that he had been left with his toenails. Typically, he would have been appalled at his nudity outside of intimacy, but between the pain in his head and the alien language floating in front of his eyes, the best response he could muster was to paraphrase one of his favorite movie lines.

"Looks like I picked the wrong week to stop wearing plaid."

It wasn't perfect. It wasn't even close to clever. But it was the only line his brain could reach for when the universe had just pantsed him and told him his flannel was a biohazard. Leslie Nielsen would have probably approved of it, given Charlie's current situation. Even panic had been left far behind him by now. All he felt was a resigned numbness, a sense of fatalism and he just wanted to get this over with and wake up in his trailer wondering what he had eaten to give him this nightmare.

His musing was interrupted by a strange, burning, squirming (?) sensation in his left shoulder. He had torn that rotator cuff years ago when a tree had suddenly moved in a way that he hadn't expected and, like a new guy, his reflexes had tried to catch the fall. He quickly focused on the text again.

Charlie rolled his shoulder, amazed that the feeling of gravel in that joint was gone. His manager at the time had rolled his eyes, but it was expected. The rest of the team had given him given him hell for years about it as well. They all had the surgery scars. Lumberjacking was a hard life and not a one of them had come away from it without pain and scars. Even the ribbing was more evidence of acceptance.

But for some odd reason the gravel in his shoulder had been the last reminder that any of it had been real. The team. The job sites. The ribbing. Even the scratched Airplane! DVD that followed him from site to site.

All gone.

He rolled his shoulder again.

"Great, now I'm doing hysterics," Charlie muttered to himself. "What next? Loafers and poetry?"

Another line in the text caught his attention. He had to scroll up in the text to find it again. He hadn't known that he could "scroll" on the text, which was a novelty in and of itself.

But, he found the line he was looking for. Precancerous cells? He had cancer?
